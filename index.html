Burmese Python Care Sheet

BURMESE PYTHON CARE

Cheat Sheet:

  • Heating: Warm Side +/- 90°F / Cool Side +/- 80°F
  • Feeding: Meal Width = Snake Width at Thickest Point
  • Housing: Enclosure L + W = snake L (bare minimum)
  • Humidity: 65-70%
  • Handling: Support Body Weight, Guide Head, Do Not Smell Like Rat
  • Safety: Nibble or Pinch end of tail = Stop Constriction. Mouthwash/Hand Sanitizer in Mouth = Release Bite
  • Motorcycling: No Don't

More Information:

Heating

Maintain a warm side (around 90°F) and a cool side (around 80°F), so that your pet can thermoregulate and always find a comfortable spot. For a young snake (or any which uses hides) make sure there are hides on both sides.

Food and Water

Food should be pre-killed whenever possible, and be roughly the size of the thickest part of your snakes body. As babies they can be fed once every 7-10 days, but it's important to not overfeed as they mature. An over-fed Burmese python will grow too quickly and become obese, which causes health problems and shortens their life span. When feeding adults, it's okay to let a couple weeks go by between meals sometimes if your pet is getting too chunky! This way their body is not in a constant state of digestion. It's ideal to provide fresh purified water for your snake to drink and if possible soak in as desired. Water usually needs to be changed at least once a week.

Housing

Provide a large enough enclosure for your snake to be able to fully stretch out if possible, or at least to be no longer than the width plus one end of their enclosure. An adult Burmese python could potentially require 8' x 4' or larger. Keep in mind also that females grow larger and heavier than males! Be sure to do a thorough cleaning at least once a month, while spot cleaning as needed.

Humidity

65-70% humidy is usually good, although I sometimes raise humidity while a snake is in shed. Burmese pythons are prone to respiratory illness and need good humidity but also clean air.

Shedding

Maintaining correct humidity usually helps shedding go smoothly. Having enough water for the snake to soak in is also helpful, and they often enjoy water! If any shed does get stuck, I've had success with giving a warm bath for 30 minutes or less.

Handling and Safety

Burmese pythons are usually awesome snakes to handle. Their huge size and laid back attitudes make them incredible reptile ambassadors! As is the case with many snakes, if you support their weight and are gentle with them they will be gentle with you. Mine is often happy to just lay on my lap. Babies and youngsters can be defensive, but gentle and consistent time with them will be rewarded. That being said, large constrictors are incredibly powerful and potentially dangerous in the wrong circumstances. Get to know your individual very well. Recognize any changes in their behavior. When you are educated and attentive, surprises are much less likely to happen! When opening your pythons enclosure, always assume that it is hungry and hoping you bring food. Watch for jerky, and possibly faster than usual movements that seem intensely focused. It is always a good idea to tap your snake or move it gentley with a snake hook or similar item to let him or her know that this isn't feeding time!

If you are mistaken for food, your snake may strike and latch on tight while looping coils around whatever part of you it can. Rather than releasing you and realizing it's mistake, your snake may seem "stuck" biting into you and squeezing, as if it doesn't know how to stop. If you have a large adult, you should never be feeding or handling it alone. If you do end up in this position, do not fight the snake or try to pry it's mouth off of you. Instead, you have a few tried and true options to minimize harm to you or your confused pet! You or your handling partner can pour mouthwash or hand sanitizer on the mouth of the snake to snap them out of feeding mode. To quickly stop constriction, you can nibble or pinch the end of the snakes tail!
